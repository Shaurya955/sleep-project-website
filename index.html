Sleep Deprivation & Its Associated Health Risks

Examining how sleep patterns relate to BMI, physical activity, ethnicity, and birth country using CDC NHANES data.

Shaurya Arora · Clara de Bellard · Akshat Sonthalia · DS4200, Spring 2025

Introduction

Dating back to the 1800s, there has been a sense that productivity equals virtue, where hard work became tied to moral worth. This concept continued growing throughout the 1900s, when success was framed as only achievable through relentless effort. These ideas gave power to phrases like "Work hard, play hard," which cultivated competitive corporate environments where busyness became a status symbol.

As social media took hold, platforms like Instagram and LinkedIn began glamorizing productivity and the idea of "no days off" and "Rise and grind." But it all changed in 2020. After COVID-19, adults in the United States found themselves at home with long hours, less work, yet more stress. An awareness of accelerated burnout started to take place, and Gen Z began to reject long-standing "grind culture." Young adults started to become interested in work-life balance and mental health priorities.

This project deep-dives into sleep patterns across U.S. adults to identify how sleep relates to associated health risks, including elevated BMI and physical activity levels, and how demographic factors like ethnicity and birth country shape these patterns.

Data Description

This project utilizes publicly available data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), specifically the NHANES (National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey) dataset. NHANES combines interview and physical examination data and is designed to assess the health and nutritional status of adults in the United States.

The primary variable of interest is weekday sleep hours. Additional variables include BMI, ethnicity, birth country, gender, work activity levels (vigorous and moderate days/minutes), hours worked per week, school attendance, and hypertension diagnosis age.

Source

CDC NHANES, accessible via data.cdc.gov. Nationally representative cross-sectional survey combining interviews and physical exams.

Key Variables

Weekday sleep hours, BMI, ethnicity, birth country, gender, work activity days, hours worked per week, school attendance, hypertension age.

Preprocessing

Missing values removed. Sleep binned into 1-hour categories. Sleep categories: Short (<7h), Recommended (7–9h), Long (>9h), Very Long (>12h).

Visualizations

Figure 1 · Altair (Python)

BMI vs. Weekday Sleep Hours

Takeaway: There is a clear negative trend between sleep hours and BMI — participants sleeping fewer hours on weekdays tend to have higher BMIs. The regression line shows those sleeping 2–3 hours average a BMI above 30 (obese range), while those sleeping longer trend lower. This supports the hypothesis that shorter sleep duration is a risk factor for elevated BMI.

Figure 2 · Altair (Python)

Average BMI by Weekday Sleep Hours and Ethnicity

Takeaway: Ethnicity meaningfully moderates the sleep–BMI relationship. Mexican American and Non-Hispanic Black participants show higher average BMIs across most sleep durations. Both very short (2–3 hrs) and very long (14–15 hrs) sleep are associated with the highest BMIs across groups, suggesting a non-linear relationship rather than a simple one.

Figure 3 · Matplotlib (Python)

Weekday Sleep Hours by Moderate Work Activity

Takeaway: People who engage in more days of moderate work activity per week tend to sleep slightly fewer hours. Median sleep hours declines from ~7.5 hrs (1 day/week) toward ~7.2 hrs (5–6 days/week). This confirms that heavier physical work schedules are associated with reduced sleep time.

Summary & Findings

Our five visualizations collectively show that sleep deprivation in the U.S. is shaped by work demands, socioeconomic status, ethnicity, and birth country. Three key conclusions emerge from the data:

01

Work Hours Reduce Sleep

Hours worked per week and days of moderate work activity are the strongest negative predictors of sleep duration. Reducing excessive work demands could directly improve population sleep health.

02

Ethnicity Shapes Sleep & BMI

The sleep–BMI relationship varies by ethnicity. Non-Hispanic Black and Mexican American participants face higher average BMIs across sleep categories, pointing to structural and social determinants beyond individual behavior.

03

Acculturation Affects Sleep

Mexico-born participants sleep more and are more likely to get recommended hours than U.S.-born counterparts. Exposure to American work norms appears to erode healthy sleep habits over time.

Future directions: Incorporating depression and hypertension outcomes from NHANES would complete the health risk picture. Time-series analysis could show whether post-pandemic awareness of burnout has actually improved population sleep over recent years.
