Sleep Deprivation and Its Associated Health Risks

Exploring how sleep patterns are associated with BMI, depression, blood pressure, physical health, and other health-related factors.

Introduction Data Visualizations Findings References

Introduction

Sleep plays an important role in both physical and mental health. In recent years, sleep deprivation has become a growing public health concern as stress, burnout, and demanding lifestyles have become more common. At the same time, awareness of work-life balance and mental health has increased, especially among younger adults.

This project examines how reduced sleep is associated with health risks such as elevated BMI, depression, hypertension, and poor physical health. Our goal is to identify which variables appear most connected to sleep and to show how these relationships vary across different groups and behaviors.

Data

This project uses publicly available CDC health data focused on short sleep duration and related health outcomes. The primary variable of interest is short sleep duration, defined as fewer than 7 hours of sleep per 24-hour period. Additional variables of interest include obesity prevalence, diagnosed hypertension, depression prevalence, and poor physical health indicators.

The main task of this project is to explore patterns in sleep and understand how shorter sleep duration may be associated with negative health outcomes. The visualizations below move from broad overview patterns to more specific comparisons so the story becomes progressively more detailed.

Summary of Findings

Overall, the visualizations suggest that sleep is related to multiple health outcomes rather than one isolated factor. Reduced sleep appears to align with worse physical and mental health indicators across several parts of the analysis, including BMI, depression, and other associated measures.

The project also shows that these patterns may vary across different demographic and behavioral groups, which suggests that sleep deprivation is shaped by a combination of health, lifestyle, and social factors rather than a single cause.

In the future, this project could be extended by examining trends over time in more detail, including additional age groups, or comparing whether these relationships remain consistent across regions and populations. More detailed longitudinal analysis could also help clarify whether poor sleep leads to worse health outcomes, or whether some of these relationships are mutually reinforcing.
