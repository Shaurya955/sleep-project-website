Sleep Deprivation and Its Associated Health Risks

Analyzing how sleep patterns are related to BMI, depression, hypertension, and overall physical health.

Introduction

In recent years, sleep has become an important public health concern. As work pressure, stress, and burnout have increased, especially after COVID-19, more people have started to recognize the importance of work-life balance and mental health. However, sleep deprivation remains common among young adults.

This project explores how sleep patterns are associated with different health outcomes. Specifically, we examine whether shorter sleep duration is linked to higher BMI, depression, hypertension, and poor physical health. Our goal is to identify key relationships and better understand how sleep impacts overall well-being.

Data

This project uses publicly available data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). The dataset contains information about sleep duration and various health indicators across the United States.

The primary variable of interest is short sleep duration, defined as fewer than 7 hours of sleep per day. Additional variables include obesity prevalence, diagnosed hypertension, depression prevalence, and days of poor physical health. These variables allow us to explore how sleep relates to both physical and mental health outcomes.

Project Goal

The goal of this project is to analyze patterns in sleep behavior and understand how reduced sleep may be linked to negative health outcomes. We use visualizations to explore correlations, identify trends, and compare different groups.

Summary of Findings

Overall, the analysis suggests that sleep is closely related to multiple health outcomes. Shorter sleep duration appears to be associated with higher risks of physical and mental health issues such as obesity and depression.

These findings highlight the importance of sleep in maintaining overall health. Future work could explore trends over time, differences across regions, and additional behavioral factors.
