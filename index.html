Sleep Deprivation and Its Associated Health Risks

Exploring how sleep patterns relate to BMI, depression, blood pressure, and other health outcomes.

Introduction Data Visualizations Findings References

Introduction

Sleep plays a critical role in both physical and mental health. In recent years, growing awareness of burnout, stress, and work-life balance has made sleep an important public health topic.

This project examines how sleep deprivation is associated with health risks such as elevated BMI, depression, hypertension, and poor physical health. Our goal is to identify which health indicators appear most connected to sleep and to show how these patterns vary across different groups.

Data

This project uses publicly available CDC health data. The primary variable of interest is short sleep duration, defined as fewer than 7 hours of sleep per 24-hour period. Additional variables of interest include obesity, diagnosed hypertension, depression prevalence, and poor physical health.

The overall task of this project is to explore patterns in sleep and understand how reduced sleep may be associated with negative health outcomes.

Summary of Findings

Overall, the visualizations suggest that sleep is connected to multiple health outcomes rather than a single isolated factor. Shorter sleep appears to align with worse physical and mental health indicators in several parts of the analysis.

In the future, this project could be extended by examining changes over time in more detail, comparing additional age groups, or testing whether these relationships remain consistent across different regions and populations.
