Sleep Deprivation and Its Associated Health Risks

Analyzing how sleep patterns relate to BMI, depression, and overall health.

Introduction

Sleep has become an important public health concern in recent years. As stress, burnout, and demanding lifestyles have increased, more individuals—especially young adults—experience sleep deprivation.

This project explores how sleep duration is associated with health outcomes such as BMI, depression, and physical health. The goal is to identify patterns and understand how reduced sleep impacts overall well-being.

Data

This project uses publicly available data from the CDC. The dataset includes sleep duration and health-related variables across the United States.

The main variable is short sleep duration (less than 7 hours). Additional variables include obesity, hypertension, depression, and physical health.

Visualizations

Takeaway: This chart shows the variables most strongly associated with sleep. Positive correlations suggest structured routines may improve sleep, while negative correlations indicate increased workload may reduce sleep.

Takeaway: This scatter plot shows a slight negative relationship between sleep and BMI, suggesting that lower sleep may be linked to higher body mass.

3. BMI by Sleep and Ethnicity

Takeaway: This heatmap shows how BMI varies across sleep levels and demographic groups. Lower sleep durations generally show higher BMI values.

4. Sleep vs Work Activity

Takeaway: Sleep remains relatively stable across activity levels, but slight decreases suggest higher workload may reduce sleep.

5. Sleep Category Distribution

Takeaway: This chart shows how sleep categories vary across gender and birth country, highlighting differences in sleep patterns among groups.

6. Interactive D3 Chart

Takeaway: This interactive chart allows users to explore how variables relate to sleep using sorting and hover interaction.

Summary

The analysis shows that sleep is strongly connected to multiple health outcomes. Reduced sleep appears to be associated with higher BMI and poorer health indicators.

Future work could explore trends over time and differences across regions or age groups.
